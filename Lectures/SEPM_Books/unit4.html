üíº IT Project Management

üìñ Definition: IT Project Management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to information technology activities to meet project requirements. It involves planning, organizing, and managing resources to bring about the successful completion of specific IT project goals and objectives.
üí° In Simple Terms: IT Project Management is like being the captain of a ship carrying valuable cargo (software). You need to navigate through various challenges (technical issues, deadlines, budget constraints), keep your crew motivated (team management), and ensure the cargo reaches its destination safely and on time (successful project delivery).

PAs (Project Attributes)

üè∑Ô∏è Project Attributes are the defining characteristics and parameters that describe a project. They establish the boundaries and expectations for what needs to be accomplished.
üéØ Essential Project Attributes:

  • Quality: Standards and acceptance criteria the deliverables must meet
  • Resources: People, equipment, and materials required
  • Risk: Potential issues and uncertainties that could impact the project
  • Stakeholders: Individuals or groups with interest in the project

üöÄ Project Management Frameworks & Methodologies

üåç The Big Picture: Different projects need different approaches. Just like you wouldn't use the same strategy to build a rocket ship as you would to bake a cake, different project management frameworks suit different types of projects. Some are flexible and adaptive (Agile), while others are structured and sequential (PRINCE2).

Agile Methodologies

üîÑ Agile is an iterative and incremental approach to project management that emphasizes flexibility, collaboration, customer feedback, and rapid delivery of working software. Instead of planning everything upfront, Agile projects adapt to changes and evolve through continuous learning.
üé® Analogy: Agile is like sketching a painting. You start with a rough outline, add layers of detail with each iteration, and continuously step back to see if it looks right. You can adjust colors, composition, and details as you go, rather than planning every brushstroke before starting.

1. Scrum Framework

üèâ Scrum - The Most Popular Agile Framework

üí° What is Scrum? Scrum is like playing rugby (where the name comes from). The team works together in short, intense bursts (called Sprints) to move the ball (project) forward. After each play, the team huddles to plan the next move, adapting their strategy based on what just happened.

Key Concepts:

Concept Description Duration/Details
Sprint Time-boxed iteration where work is completed 1-4 weeks (typically 2 weeks)
Product Backlog Prioritized list of features and requirements Maintained by Product Owner
Sprint Backlog Tasks selected for current sprint Committed by Development Team
Increment Working product delivered at end of sprint Potentially shippable
Definition of Done Criteria for completed work Agreed by entire team

Scrum Roles:

üëî Product Owner

The "What" Person

  • Defines product vision
  • Manages product backlog
  • Prioritizes features
  • Accepts completed work
  • Represents stakeholders

üéØ Scrum Master

The "How" Person

  • Facilitates Scrum process
  • Removes impediments
  • Shields team from distractions
  • Coaches team on Agile
  • Organizes ceremonies

üë• Development Team

The "Do" People

  • Self-organizing team (3-9 members)
  • Cross-functional skills
  • Estimates work
  • Commits to sprint goals
  • Delivers increment

Scrum Ceremonies/Events:

üé¨ Sprint Planning

Duration: 2-4 hours

  • What will be delivered?
  • How will work be done?
  • Team selects backlog items
  • Creates sprint backlog

Duration: 15 minutes

  • What did I do yesterday?
  • What will I do today?
  • Any blockers/impediments?

Duration: 1-2 hours

  • Get stakeholder feedback
  • Update product backlog
  • Discuss what's next

üîÑ Sprint Retrospective

  • What could be improved?
  • Action items for next sprint

Each sprint delivers working software that can be tested and demonstrated!

2. Kanban

üìã Kanban - Visualize Your Workflow

üí° What is Kanban? Kanban is like a conveyor belt in a factory. Work items move through different stages (columns) on a board, and you can see exactly what's being worked on, what's waiting, and what's done. The key rule: limit how much work is "in progress" at once to avoid bottlenecks.
üçî Analogy: Think of a fast-food restaurant. Orders (tasks) flow through stages: "Order Placed" ‚Üí "Preparing" ‚Üí "Cooking" ‚Üí "Quality Check" ‚Üí "Serving". They limit how many burgers cook at once to ensure quality. That's Kanban!

Core Principles:

üéØ Kanban Principles:
  • Visualize Workflow: Make work visible on a board
  • Limit Work in Progress (WIP): Restrict tasks per column
  • Manage Flow: Optimize speed through the system
  • Make Policies Explicit: Clear rules for moving cards
  • Continuous Improvement: Always optimize the process
  • Feedback Loops: Regular reviews and adjustments

3. Extreme Programming (XP)

‚ö° Extreme Programming - Engineering Excellence

üí° What is XP? Extreme Programming takes good software engineering practices and "turns them up to 11." If code reviews are good, do them continuously (pair programming). If testing is good, test continuously (TDD). If integration is good, integrate multiple times a day (CI). XP is all about technical excellence and quality.
üé∏ Analogy: If traditional development is like practicing guitar alone, XP is like jamming with a band. You're constantly collaborating, getting immediate feedback, and adjusting your playing in real-time to create better music together.

Core Practices:

üë• Pair Programming

Two developers, one computer

  • Driver: writes code
  • Navigator: reviews and thinks ahead
  • Switch roles frequently
  • Better code quality
  • Knowledge sharing

üß™ Test-Driven Development (TDD)

Write tests before code

  • Red: Write failing test
  • Green: Write minimum code to pass
  • Refactor: Improve code
  • Repeat cycle
  • High test coverage

üîÑ Continuous Integration

Integrate code frequently

  • Commit code multiple times daily
  • Automated builds
  • Run all tests
  • Fix broken builds immediately
  • Always deployable

üî® Refactoring

Continuously improve design

  • Improve code structure
  • Remove duplication
  • Enhance readability
  • Keep tests passing
  • Small, frequent changes

üéØ Simple Design

Keep it as simple as possible

  • Passes all tests
  • No duplication
  • Expresses intent clearly
  • Minimal classes/methods
  • YAGNI principle

üë§ Collective Code Ownership

Everyone owns all code

  • Any developer can modify any code
  • No code silos
  • Shared responsibility
  • Better team flexibility
  • Reduces bottlenecks
üéØ XP Values:
  • Communication: Constant face-to-face interaction
  • Simplicity: Do the simplest thing that works
  • Feedback: Quick, continuous feedback loops
  • Courage: Willingness to refactor and change
  • Respect: Team members respect each other's contributions

PRINCE2 Methodology

üëë PRINCE2 - PRojects IN Controlled Environments

üìñ PRINCE2 is a process-based project management methodology that provides a structured approach to managing projects. It's widely used in the UK government and many organizations worldwide. Unlike Agile, PRINCE2 is more formal and document-driven.
üí° What is PRINCE2? PRINCE2 is like following a detailed instruction manual for assembling furniture. Every step is documented, every role is defined, and there are clear checkpoints to ensure you're on the right track. It's very structured and controlled.

7 Principles of PRINCE2:

Principle Description
1. Continued Business Justification Project must remain viable and aligned with business objectives throughout
2. Learn from Experience Lessons are captured and applied from previous projects
3. Defined Roles and Responsibilities Clear organization structure with defined roles
4. Manage by Stages Project divided into manageable stages with reviews
5. Manage by Exception Defined tolerances for delegation and escalation
6. Focus on Products Emphasis on quality and definition of deliverables
7. Tailor to Suit Environment Methodology adapted to project size and complexity

7 Processes of PRINCE2:

1. Starting Up a Project (SU)

  • Appoint project team
  • Design and appoint project management team
  • Prepare outline business case
  • Select approach and assemble project brief

2. Directing a Project (DP)

  • Authorize initiation
  • Authorize project
  • Authorize stage or exception plan
  • Give ad hoc direction
  • Authorize project closure

3. Initiating a Project (IP)

  • Prepare risk management strategy
  • Prepare quality management strategy
  • Prepare communication strategy
  • Set up project controls
  • Create project plan
  • Refine business case
  • Assemble project initiation documentation

4. Controlling a Stage (CS)

  • Authorize work package
  • Review work package status
  • Receive completed work packages
  • Review stage status
  • Report highlights
  • Capture and examine issues
  • Escalate issues and risks

5. Managing Product Delivery (MP)

  • Accept work package
  • Execute work package
  • Deliver work package

6. Managing Stage Boundary (SB)

  • Plan next stage
  • Update project plan
  • Update business case
  • Report stage end
  • Produce exception plan (if needed)

7. Closing a Project (CP)

  • Prepare planned closure
  • Prepare premature closure
  • Hand over products
  • Evaluate project
  • Recommend project closure

DevOps

üîÑ DevOps - Development Meets Operations

ü§ù DevOps is a set of practices that combines software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops) to shorten the development lifecycle and provide continuous delivery of high-quality software. It's about breaking down silos and fostering collaboration.
üí° What is DevOps? Imagine developers and operations teams used to work in separate buildings and only talked through formal meetings. DevOps is like tearing down the wall between them and having them work side-by-side in the same room, using the same tools, and sharing the same goals.
üè≠ Analogy: Think of a car factory. DevOps is like having the designers who create the car work directly with the assembly line workers. The designers understand manufacturing constraints, and the workers can suggest design improvements. This collaboration makes better cars, faster.

DevOps Core Practices:

üîÑ Continuous Integration (CI)

  • Frequent code commits
  • Automated builds
  • Automated testing
  • Quick feedback on changes
  • Early detection of issues

üöÄ Continuous Delivery/Deployment (CD)

  • Automated deployment pipeline
  • Release to production frequently
  • Small, incremental changes
  • Reduced deployment risk
  • Faster time to market

üìä Monitoring & Logging

  • Real-time application monitoring
  • Centralized logging
  • Performance metrics
  • Alerting and notification
  • Proactive issue detection

üèóÔ∏è Infrastructure as Code (IaC)

  • Automated infrastructure provisioning
  • Version-controlled configs
  • Reproducible environments
  • Faster setup and scaling
  • Reduced manual errors

üì¶ Microservices Architecture

  • Decompose into small services
  • Independent deployment
  • Loosely coupled components
  • Technology flexibility
  • Easier scaling

ü§ù Collaboration & Communication

  • Shared responsibility
  • Cross-functional teams
  • ChatOps and collaboration tools
  • Blameless post-mortems
  • Continuous improvement culture

üîç Systems View of Project Management

üåê Systems View means looking at a project as a complete system with interconnected parts, rather than isolated components. Changes in one area affect other areas - just like in a living organism, everything is connected.
üß† Analogy: Think of a project like the human body. You can't just focus on the heart (development) and ignore the lungs (testing) or the brain (planning). All systems must work together harmoniously. If one system fails, the whole body suffers.

Three Key Aspects of Systems View

1. üîó System Integration

Connecting the Pieces

  • All project components are interconnected
  • Changes ripple through the system
  • Must consider dependencies
  • Integration points are critical
  • Holistic problem-solving approach

2. üåç External Environment

Context Matters

  • Projects exist within organizations
  • Influenced by market conditions
  • Regulatory and compliance requirements
  • Stakeholder expectations
  • Competitive landscape

3. ‚öñÔ∏è Balance & Trade-offs

The Iron Triangle

  • Scope vs. Time vs. Cost
  • Optimizing one affects others
  • Must make informed trade-offs
  • Quality is the center point
  • Balance stakeholder priorities

üéÆ Example - Game Development Project:

Scenario: CEO wants to add multiplayer mode to a game 1 week before launch

Systems View Analysis:

  • Time Impact: Need 3 more months of development

  • Quality Impact: Rushing will increase bugs and technical debt
  • Risk Impact: Missing launch date, competitor advantage, team burnout
Trade-off Decision: Either delay launch by 3 months, increase budget by $80K, or save multiplayer for Version 2.0

üìÖ Project Scheduling and Tracking

üåç The Big Picture: Project scheduling is like planning a complex journey with multiple stops, different modes of transport, and various activities. You need to figure out what to do, in what order, how long each part takes, and which activities are critical for reaching your destination on time.

Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

üå≥ Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) is a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team. It breaks down the project into smaller, more manageable pieces called work packages.
üí° Simple Explanation: Think of WBS like breaking down "Build a House" into smaller pieces: Foundation ‚Üí Walls ‚Üí Roof ‚Üí Plumbing ‚Üí Electrical ‚Üí Interior. Each piece can then be broken down further until you have tasks small enough to estimate, assign, and track.

Critical Path Method (CPM)

üõ§Ô∏è Critical Path Method (CPM) is a technique used to identify the longest sequence of dependent activities (the critical path) that determines the minimum project duration. Activities on the critical path cannot be delayed without delaying the entire project.
üç≥ Analogy: Imagine cooking a complex meal. Some tasks can be done in parallel (chopping vegetables while rice cooks), but some must be sequential (can't frost a cake before it's baked and cooled). The critical path is the longest sequence of tasks that must be done in order - this determines when dinner will be ready!
üîë Key Concepts in CPM:
  • Critical Path: The longest path through the project network (shortest possible duration)
  • Critical Activities: Tasks on the critical path with zero slack/float
  • Float/Slack: How much an activity can be delayed without delaying the project
  • Early Start (ES): Earliest time an activity can begin
  • Early Finish (EF): Earliest time an activity can complete
  • Late Start (LS): Latest time an activity can start without delaying project
  • Late Finish (LF): Latest time an activity can finish without delaying project
  • Total Float: LF - EF (or LS - ES)
üíª Example - Software Module Development:

Activity Duration Predecessors
A: Design Database 5 days -
B: Design UI 3 days -
C: Implement Database 8 days A
D: Develop Frontend 10 days B
E: Develop Backend 12 days C
F: Integration Testing 5 days D, E

Critical Path: A ‚Üí C ‚Üí E ‚Üí F (5 + 8 + 12 + 5 = 30 days)
Non-Critical Path: B ‚Üí D ‚Üí F (3 + 10 + 5 = 18 days, has 12 days of float)

Implication: Any delay in activities A, C, E, or F will delay the entire project. Activity D could be delayed by up to 12 days without impacting the project completion.

PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique)

üìä PERT is a statistical tool used to analyze and represent the tasks involved in completing a project. Unlike CPM which uses single time estimates, PERT uses three time estimates (optimistic, most likely, pessimistic) to account for uncertainty.
üí° What Makes PERT Different? PERT recognizes that we're not always certain how long tasks will take. Instead of saying "this will take 10 days," PERT says "best case 7 days, most likely 10 days, worst case 15 days" and calculates a weighted average.

Estimate Symbol Description
Optimistic Time O Best-case scenario - everything goes perfectly
Most Likely Time M Realistic estimate - normal conditions
Pessimistic Time P Worst-case scenario - everything goes wrong

Gantt Charts

üìä Gantt Chart is a horizontal bar chart that illustrates a project schedule. It shows the start and finish dates of project elements, dependencies between tasks, and the current status of the project.
üí° Simple Explanation: A Gantt chart is like a visual timeline. Each task is a horizontal bar whose length represents its duration. You can see at a glance what should be happening when, what's dependent on what, and whether you're on track.
üéØ Gantt Chart Components:
  • Task List: All project activities listed vertically
  • Timeline: Time period shown horizontally (days, weeks, months)
  • Bars: Horizontal bars showing task duration and position in time
  • Dependencies: Arrows showing task relationships
  • Milestones: Diamond symbols marking significant points
  • Progress: Shaded portions showing completion percentage
  • Critical Path: Often highlighted in different color
  • Today Marker: Vertical line showing current date

üéØ Project Scope Management

üì¶ Project Scope Management includes the processes required to ensure that the project includes all the work required, and only the work required, to complete the project successfully. It's about defining what's in and what's out.

Scope Creep

‚ö†Ô∏è Scope Creep: The uncontrolled expansion of project scope without adjustments to time, cost, and resources. It's the #1 killer of projects!
üçï Analogy: You ordered a cheese pizza (original scope). Then someone says "let's add pepperoni!" Then "mushrooms too!" Then "and olives!" Before you know it, you're paying for a supreme pizza but still expected to pay the cheese pizza price and get it in the same time. That's scope creep!

Common Causes of Scope Creep:

  • Poor Requirements Definition: Vague or incomplete initial requirements
  • Weak Change Control: No formal process for handling changes
  • Gold Plating: Team adds features nobody asked for
  • Stakeholder Pressure: "Just one more small thing..."
  • Lack of Communication: Misunderstandings about what's included
  • No Baseline: No agreed-upon scope to compare against

Preventing Scope Creep:

1. Clear Scope Statement

  • Document what's in scope
  • Document what's out of scope
  • Get stakeholder sign-off
  • Reference throughout project

2. Change Control Process

  • Formal change request form
  • Impact analysis (time, cost, risk)
  • Approval authority defined
  • Track all changes

3. Regular Communication

  • Frequent stakeholder updates
  • Manage expectations
  • Clarify misunderstandings early
  • Reinforce scope boundaries

4. "No" is a Valid Answer

  • Learn to push back
  • Explain impact of changes
  • Offer alternatives (future version)
  • Protect team and timeline
